Fortis Title & Escrow · AI Setup Runbook

Claude for the owner, Qualia for the files

Qualia's AI already works the closings — this setup doesn't touch that, and it adds no systems and asks nothing of the team. This is one assistant for Michael: his voice, his decisions, his deals, his playbook. Work top to bottom; every gray box is a prompt — hit Copy, paste into Claude, follow what comes back. Phases 1–3 are today's setup; Phases 4–6 are how Michael uses it every week. Mid-session help lives on the Setup Copilot page.

Two rules before anything else. (1) No client Social Security numbers, bank account numbers, or wire instructions go into any AI chat — ever. Names, addresses, file numbers, and stages are fine. (2) The AI drafts and advises; a person decides and sends. It never states binding closing figures and never touches money.

Phase 3 · Voice & daily habits~30 min

Make it sound like Michael, then make it useful daily

Teach it Michael's voice

Claude reads a stack of his real sent emails, writes down the patterns as a short voice guide, Michael corrects it, and the corrected version gets saved in the Project. Every draft after that uses it.

  • If the email connector is on: run the first prompt below as-is.
  • If not: copy 10–15 of Michael's sent emails into the chat first (a mix — agents, clients, lenders; skip any containing account numbers), then run the same prompt.
  • Read the guide together. Michael crosses out anything that isn't him and adds what's missing — this edit is the most important part.
  • Save the corrected version as a doc in Project knowledge named Michael's Voice.
Prompt · Build the voice guide
Read 15 emails I've sent to real estate agents, lenders, and clients (from my sent mail, or the samples I've pasted below). Ignore any sensitive numbers. Then write a one-page voice guide describing how I actually write, covering: how I open and close emails, typical sentence length, how formal or casual I am, phrases I use often, how I deliver bad news or push back, and how I sign off. Format it as instructions to a writing assistant ("Michael opens with..., Michael never says..."). End with three things I apparently never do, so drafts avoid them. I'll correct anything you get wrong before we save it.
Prompt · Voice check (run on any draft)
Check the draft below against the "Michael's Voice" guide in this project. Go line by line and flag anything that doesn't sound like me — words I wouldn't use, sentences that are too stiff or too long, anything that smells like AI wrote it. Then give me the corrected version. Finally, show the first two sentences side by side: the generic way an assistant would write them, and my way, so I can see the difference.

Phase 4 · The owner's deskuse weekly

The work that happens outside Qualia

Qualia works the closings. This is everything else that lands on an owner's desk — contracts, vendors, decisions, relationships. This is where Claude earns its seat.

Prompt · Read a contract or agreement
I'm attaching a contract/agreement someone wants me to sign (vendor, lease, service agreement, renewal). In plain English: (1) what it commits us to, in money and obligations; (2) every date that matters — term, auto-renewal, cancellation window, price escalations; (3) the clauses that favor the other side or could bite us later; (4) the questions I should ask before signing; (5) anything a lawyer should look at. This is preparation, not legal advice.
Prompt · Think through a decision
Here's a business decision I'm weighing: [DESCRIBE IT — hire someone, drop a vendor, raise a fee, open a second location, spend on marketing]. Interview me one question at a time, no more than five questions, to understand what actually matters here. Then give me: the strongest honest case for each option, what you would do in my seat and why, and the one piece of information that would change your answer. Push back if I'm talking myself into something.

Agent outreach — filling the coffee calendar

Growth for a title shop is relationships, and relationships start with a lunch. Claude turns "I should reach out to more agents" into messages actually sent: it drafts the asks in Michael's voice, plans the week's touches, and keeps the follow-ups from dying. (Coffee and lunch are normal marketing and RESPA-fine — the line that's never crossed is anything tied to referral volume.)

Prompt · Coffee/lunch ask — new agent
Draft a short message from me to [AGENT NAME], a real estate agent at [BROKERAGE], asking to grab coffee or lunch. Context: [HOW I KNOW OF THEM — met at a closing, mutual contact, saw their listing activity, "cold"]. My voice guide applies. Rules: it's about getting to know them and their business, not a pitch about Fortis; one specific, low-pressure ask ("coffee near your office some morning next week?"); under 90 words. Give me an email version and a text version, and one alternate opener in case the first feels off.
Prompt · Re-engage an agent who's gone quiet
[AGENT NAME] used to send us business but it's been a while — last contact was roughly [WHEN / WHAT]. Draft a warm, no-guilt check-in from me asking to catch up over lunch. Do not mention order volume, do not ask why they stopped, do not sell. Just a genuine reconnect from one professional to another, in my voice, under 80 words. Email and text versions.
Prompt · Plan the week's outreach
Here's my list of agents and loan officers I want to build or maintain relationships with, with a note on where each stands: [PASTE NAMES + A FEW WORDS EACH — e.g., "Jane Smith, Howard Hanna, met once, promising" / "Bob Lee, sent 3 deals last year, quiet lately"]. Pick the five I should touch this week, tell me why those five, and draft each message — coffee ask, check-in, or congratulations, whatever fits. My voice, short, nothing salesy, nothing tied to referrals. I'll send them myself.
Prompt · After the meeting — follow-up & next touch
I just had coffee/lunch with [AGENT NAME]. Quick notes on what we talked about: [TWO OR THREE ROUGH NOTES — kids' names, their market focus, a problem they mentioned, anything I promised]. Draft: (1) a same-day thank-you text in my voice referencing something real from the conversation; (2) if I promised anything, the message or action that delivers it; (3) a suggested next touch and when — a month out, tied to something specific about them, not generic. Save the personal details in your reply so I can paste them into a note for next time.

Making the outreach automatic

The target is zero-friction, not zero-Michael: everything arrives prepared; nothing goes out as him. With the connectors from Phase 1 on, the Monday routine looks like this:

  • Microsoft 365 connector: the re-engage and follow-up drafts pull from the real Outlook history — no pasting.
  • Times in the ask: if calendar access shows up in the connector, coffee asks go out with his real open slots; if not, Michael pastes his availability into the prompt — ten seconds either way.
  • The Monday trigger: look in the app for a scheduled-tasks feature — if his version has it, schedule "plan my outreach" for Monday 8 a.m. so the five drafts are waiting with his coffee. If not, a phone reminder that opens the app does the same job: the prompt is one library trigger away ("plan my outreach").
  • Never automated: the send. Ten minutes on Monday — read, tweak, send five messages — and the relationship pipeline stays warm all year.
Prompt · Prep for a referral partner meeting
I'm meeting [NAME], a [real estate agent / loan officer / builder] at [COMPANY]. Here's what I know about them and our history: [A SENTENCE OR TWO, OR "not much"]. Give me: three talking points that are about their business, not mine; two questions worth asking; and a short follow-up note skeleton for after. Keep it relationship and service quality — nothing that ties value to referrals.
Phase 5 · The deal screenwhenever a pitch shows up

A skeptical second opinion, on demand

People pitch successful business owners constantly — syndications, franchises, "opportunities." The standing habit: before any money moves, the pitch goes through the screen. Claude doesn't say yes or no; it arms Michael with the questions and the red flags so the promoter has to earn it.

Prompt · Screen an investment pitch
Someone wants me to put money into the deal described in the attached materials (or pasted below). Be my skeptical partner — assume the pitch is designed to sell me, and stress-test it:
1. List the key claims (price, returns, occupancy, track record, timeline) and for each, say how I could independently verify it — public records, an appraisal, a third party.
2. Flag anything that matches known patterns of weak or predatory deals: pressure deadlines, guaranteed-sounding returns, fees that contradict the pitch, thin sponsor track record, missing documents.
3. Give me the exact list of questions to send the promoter, and the documents I should demand before any further conversation.
4. Tell me what an experienced investor would want to see that is NOT in these materials.
Do not tell me whether to invest — that's my call and my advisors'. Arm me.

One more habit that costs nothing: anything with a wire deadline attached gets an automatic pause. Real deals survive a week of questions; bad ones can't afford one.

Phase 6 · The playbookone page a week

Write down how Fortis actually runs

Michael's long-term goal is a business that runs — and sells — without him in the middle. Qualia holds the data; it doesn't document the judgment. The playbook is that judgment written down: how the shop opens an order, saves an angry agent, wins a builder, handles a wire-fraud scare. One page a week, built by interview — Claude asks, Michael talks, a page comes out.

  • Pick a topic each week. Start with the thing only Michael knows how to do.
  • Run the prompt, answer the questions out loud or typed — it takes ten minutes.
  • Correct the page it writes, then save it to Project knowledge named Playbook — [topic].
  • In a year: a documented operation a manager can run from and a buyer can verify. That's the multiple.
Prompt · Playbook session
We're writing the Fortis Playbook — documenting how this shop actually runs, one page at a time. Today's topic: [E.G., how we handle an agent who's upset about a delay / how we open and assign a new order / how we spot and stop a wire-fraud attempt / how we win a new builder relationship].

Interview me one question at a time, no more than eight questions. Dig for the judgment calls, not just the steps — what I look for, when I escalate, what a new hire would get wrong. Then write a one-page SOP in plain English with: when this situation comes up, the steps, the judgment calls and how to make them, and what "done right" looks like. Title it "Playbook — [topic]". I'll correct it before we save it.
Prompt · First session — map the topics
Before we write any playbook pages: interview me for ten minutes, one question at a time, about what parts of Fortis live only in my head — the things that would break or wobble if I took a month off. Then give me a ranked list of 10–15 playbook topics, most-critical first, so we know what to write in what order.
Phase 7 · Later — Coworka few weeks in, once the habits stick

The upgrade: Claude that works with your files

Everything above happens in a chat window. Cowork is a mode in the desktop app where Claude can open a folder on the computer, read what's inside, and produce real documents — Word files, spreadsheets — instead of chat replies. Useful later for reviewing a closing folder's documents in one pass, or turning playbook pages into a formatted manual.

  • Wait until the chat habits are real — one new tool at a time.
  • Make one folder called Claude Workspace; that's the only folder Claude gets pointed at. Copy documents in; client folders stay off limits.
  • Run the first task on a closed file, together, so nothing is at stake.
Prompt · First Cowork task (use a closed file)
The folder I've given you contains the documents from one of our closed title files. Read everything in it and give me: (1) a list of each document and what it is, in one line each; (2) anything a title agency's checklist would say is missing from a complete file; (3) every date or deadline these documents created while the file was open. Do not repeat any Social Security numbers or bank account numbers in your answer. Save the result as a Word document called "File Review" in the same folder.
Standing rules

The guardrails

  • Claude complements Qualia — it never duplicates the production work Qualia's AI already does.
  • AI drafts and advises. A human decides and sends. Every time.
  • Never in a chat: SSNs, bank or account numbers, wire instructions.
  • Never anything of value to a referral source — for referrals or reviews.
  • On deals: Claude arms the questions; Michael and his advisors make the call. Anything with a wire deadline gets a pause.
